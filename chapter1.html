Two More Days

Chapter 1 — Main Story

Ten minutes left in class.

Around him, people were packing bags, tapping pens, whispering to each other. Nobody was listening to the teacher anymore. That was fine. Aaron wasn't either.

He was watching the clock.

Again.

He didn't know when the habit started — two weeks ago, maybe three. It just happened. He'd be eating lunch, walking down a hallway, sitting in this exact seat in this exact classroom, and suddenly his eyes would already be on the nearest clock before he'd made any decision to look. Like blinking. Like breathing. He didn't choose it.

Why do I keep doing this.

He didn't have an answer.

The second hand ticked forward. Smooth, steady, normal.

Then it jumped.

Not forward — sideways. For just a half-second, the hand seemed to be in two places at once, overlapping itself like a double-exposed photograph. Wrong. He didn't have a better word for it. Just wrong.

Then it was gone. The clock looked normal again. Ticking like nothing happened.

...Huh.

Something in him wanted to reach toward that wrongness — like there was a question he needed to ask, except he had no idea what the question was. The feeling slipped away before he could catch it.

Whatever.

The bell rang.

— ◆ —

Eren was already waiting at the school gate, bag on his shoulder, looking like someone who'd left the moment class ended.

"Finally," he said when Aaron walked up. "Bell rang like two minutes ago."

"Bell just rang."

"For B-class, sure." Eren grinned. "You're always the last one out lately. What are you even doing in there?"

Before Aaron could answer, his sister jogged up behind them, slightly out of breath, her A-class badge crooked on her collar.

"Sorry, sorry — Ms. Hara kept us for the graduation rehearsal again," Lily said.

"Again?" Eren groaned.

"Again." She fell into step beside Aaron, glancing at him. "You okay? You've got that look."

"What look."

"The staring-at-nothing look."

Eren pointed at her. "Yes. That. He was doing the clock thing again, wasn't he?"

Aaron sighed. "I don't even notice when I'm doing it."

"He stared at the microwave timer on Tuesday," Lily said to Eren. "It wasn't even on."

"Okay, that's kind of creepy."

"Thanks," Aaron said.

"Just saying."

They started walking — the three of them falling into the easy rhythm of a route they'd taken so many times they didn't have to think about it anymore. Past the convenience store, over the railway crossing, through the quiet part of town that looked the same every afternoon.

"Two more days," Lily said after a while, almost to herself. "Then we're actually done."

"Don't say it like it's a funeral," Eren said. "You're still going to see me. I'm not going anywhere."

"That sounded like a threat."

"Little bit, yeah."

Two more days.

Aaron didn't say anything. He just turned the thought over in his head. Two days until graduation, and then — no more countdown. No more deadline. Just life, starting, with nothing marking it.

He didn't check his watch on the walk home. He thought about it three times.

— ◆ —

Their apartment was small — two rooms, a kitchen that fit one person at a time, walls thin enough to hear the neighbor's TV at night. Lily's scholarship covered the rent. Aaron's part-time job covered everything else. It worked. It had worked since they left the children's home at the start of high school, just the two of them figuring it out, because there wasn't anyone else to do it.

They didn't talk about that part much. They didn't need to.

Lily kicked her shoes off the second the door closed.

"I'll cook tonight. You did it yesterday."

"You have study group at seven."

"I have time."

"Fine. I'll do the dishes."

"Deal."

She disappeared into the kitchen. Aaron dropped his bag in its usual spot by the door and headed to his room.

He passed the hallway clock on the way.

He stopped.

He didn't mean to. He just — stopped. Standing there, looking at it, the way he'd been looking at every clock for the past two weeks without knowing why.

Stop it.

He pulled his eyes away and kept walking. The feeling faded, like it always did. Small. Quiet. Nothing anyone would ever call important.

Two more days.

He had no idea that something inside him had been counting down to something else entirely.

— ◆ —

The apartment went quiet slowly. Kitchen light off. Hallway light off. Last, the lamp by Aaron's bed.

"Night," Lily called through the wall.

"Night. Don't stay up texting."

"No promises."

He lay in the dark and listened to the usual sounds — a car outside, the refrigerator humming, someone's TV a few doors down. Normal sounds. Ordinary night.

He closed his eyes.

He was asleep almost immediately.

— ◆ —

Fire.

Not the kind that warms you — the kind that replaces everything. The sky wasn't dark. The sky was burning. The whole world was burning, and it had been burning long enough that there was no before left to remember.

Buildings collapsed in on themselves like paper left in water. Streets ended in nothing. And everywhere — people. Too many. He couldn't look directly at them. The scale of it stopped making sense and turned into something else — just weight, pressing down on him even though he had no body here, no breath, no voice.

Blood in the rain.

And then — her.

A girl, standing completely still in the middle of all of it. She wasn't running. She wasn't fighting. She was just standing there, watching the destruction around her the way you watch rain — like it had nothing to do with her anymore. In her hands, a sword, long and double-edged, both edges catching the firelight.

Aaron tried to call out. He had no voice.

He tried to move. He had no legs.

He could only watch as, slowly, she began to turn her head toward him—

The alarm went off.

Aaron's eyes snapped open. Ceiling. His ceiling. His room, his cheap curtains, his phone buzzing off the nightstand.

He sat up. His heart was going fast enough to feel it in his teeth.

For a second, he genuinely couldn't remember where the fire had gone.

Then: morning. Sunlight. The sound of Lily starting the kettle in the kitchen.

He pressed the heel of his hand against one eye and sat there.

Was that the first time I dreamed that? Or just the first time I remembered it?

He didn't know. He wasn't sure which answer was worse.

Two more days.

— ◆ —

Graduation was forty minutes long and felt like three hours.

Aaron didn't listen to the principal's speech. He watched Lily across the auditorium — two rows away, A-class on the left side, sitting completely straight in the way she always did when she was nervous and trying not to show it.

When her name was called, she stood up so fast her chair scraped. She didn't look back at him.

She didn't need to. He always knew where she was.

When his own name was called, he didn't look for her either. Same reason.

Outside afterward was the usual chaos — caps in the air, phones out, everyone taking the same photo. Eren found them in the crowd, tie already half-undone.

"Never again," he announced.

"It's graduation," Lily said. "It only happens once."

"The principal's speech alone was twenty minutes."

"It was fine."

"It was not fine, Lily."

They went to their usual restaurant — the one they'd been going to since first year, cheap enough that splitting the bill didn't hurt, familiar enough that the owner had their order memorized. Eren raised his soda like it was something fancier.

"To never wearing that uniform again."

"To never wearing that uniform again," Lily said.

Aaron raised his glass a beat late. "...Yeah."

Eren looked at him. "You're doing the thing."

"What thing."

"The thing where you go 'yeah' like that and it means you're not actually here."

"I'm here."

Lily was already watching him, quieter. "Did you sleep okay?"

He almost said it. The fire, the bodies, the girl with the sword standing in the middle of it all. He got as far as taking a breath.

"Just tired," he said instead. "Big day."

Lily held his eyes for one second too long, then let it go. She always knew when to push and when not to.

The rest of the night was good — easy and loud the way it was supposed to be, the three of them making promises across the table that they actually meant. By the time they left it was almost eleven, the streets quiet the way small towns got late at night.

Eren split off at the corner, waving without turning around.

"Later, guys."

"Later," Lily called after him.

Aaron watched him go, then turned back to the street ahead.

Two more turns from home.

— ◆ —

They were mid-conversation — something ordinary, already half-forgotten — when the air changed.

Aaron didn't have a word for it. It wasn't cold. It wasn't a sound. It was more like pressure, right behind his eyes — in the exact same spot the clock glitch had been two days ago. His feet stopped before he told them to.

Lily glanced at him. "What—"

Two people were standing in the street ahead of them.

They hadn't walked up. They hadn't come around a corner. One moment the street was empty, and the next they were simply there — a man and a woman in clothes that didn't quite belong to anywhere Aaron recognized, watching the two of them with the calm of people who already knew exactly how this was going to go.

The woman spoke first.

"Confirmed signature. Origin: this one." Her eyes moved to Aaron. Not cruel. Just certain.

"What?" Aaron said. "What does that mean?"

"Unauthorized bending. Earth territory. Do you know how serious that is?"

"I haven't done anything. I don't even know what bending—"

"He hasn't done anything." Lily stepped half in front of Aaron — an old reflex, the kind that didn't need thinking about. "We don't know what you're talking about."

"That's not your call to make," the woman said.

There was no warning. No sound, no flash — just the sudden feeling that the air had changed its mind about gravity, and Aaron couldn't move. Something had closed around his wrists and ankles — not rope, not metal, but light, folded into something solid. Beside him, Lily went rigid. She pulled against it twice and stopped, breathing hard, her eyes wide.

This isn't real. This can't be—

Ahead of them, something was opening in the air. A tear. A gap. The streetlight hit it wrong, like the light itself didn't want to go near it.

"Save your questions for the Court," the man said.

"The what?" Aaron said. "Where are you taking us—"

"You'll see," the woman said. "Soon enough."

The gap widened. Through it: not darkness, not light. Somewhere else. Something too large to fit in a single glance.

Lily's voice, very quiet, right before they were pulled forward:

"What the hell is happening?"

Aaron didn't answer. He didn't have one.

The street closed behind them like it had never been there at all.
